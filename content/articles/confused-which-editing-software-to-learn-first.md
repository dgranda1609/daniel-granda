I have been sitting with this question for a while.

And my honest answer is a little uncomfortable, even for me to say.

I spent years -- real years, teenage years, late nights -- inside After Effects, Photoshop, and DaVinci Resolve.

When I was a kid and first figured out how to animate a solid in After Effects, I genuinely felt like I had unlocked a superpower. Like I could speak a creative language most people did not have access to.

**That feeling became my identity. Then my career.**

So it is strange to sit here and think: what if the sequence I followed is not the right one anymore?

## Here is what made me confront that question head-on.

The video attached to this post was built without touching Premiere, After Effects, or Resolve once.

The entire thing was produced using **Claude Code** -- Anthropic's AI coding assistant -- combined with **Remotion**, an open source framework for building videos with React and TypeScript.

Inside Claude Code there is a Remotion skill that lets you describe what you want and have the code written, revised, and iterated through conversation.

The two AI-generated images in the video came from prompts I wrote in about 60 seconds each.

A 43-second animation.

Brand guidelines respected.

Hard cuts, editorial motion, animated terminal sequences, dual AI image generation demos.

Done.

And here is the part I keep turning over in my head: **it was genuinely fun.**

The same kind of fun I had as a teenager discovering I could make things move on a screen.

## The uncomfortable part

I will be honest about the uncomfortable side too.

I spent years of my life learning these traditional tools. Real discipline, real craft, real time.

And it does not feel great to wonder whether that investment is going to matter less in a few more years.

That feeling is real and I am not going to pretend it is not there.

But the excitement is also real.

And for me, that is the signal worth following.

## My take for younger creators

Learn AI creative workflows first.

Use them to move fast, explore directions, and get your ideas out of your head and into the world.

Then layer in traditional tools -- After Effects, DaVinci, Resolve -- as the precision layer.

That is still where craft, technical depth, and final polish live.

**The sequence is changing. Not the destination.**

For those of us who came up the traditional way: the foundation still matters.

The intuition for timing, hierarchy, motion, and narrative does not disappear.

But holding onto the old sequence out of habit is going to cost more than learning the new one.

The landscape is changing.

**Are you in?**
