Sundance 2026 is where it became undeniable. Multiple AI-generated shorts premiered — using Sora 2 and Runway Gen-4 — that played to audiences without triggering the uncanny valley reaction that killed earlier AI video work. The temporal consistency problem, the "jitter" that made AI-generated footage look like a bad dream, has been solved at a technical level.

I've been using these tools professionally for three years. Here's the honest assessment from the production trenches.

## What Actually Got Fixed

**Temporal consistency.** This was the core problem: AI video models couldn't maintain stable subjects, environments, or motion across more than a few seconds. Every cut felt like a different shot of a different thing. Sora 2 and Runway Gen-4 addressed this through architectural improvements in how they handle motion and scene coherence. The result is footage that can hold up over 15-30 second clips.

**Native audio generation.** Sora 2, Veo 3.1, and Kling 2.6 now generate synchronized audio — sound effects, ambient audio, and in some cases dialogue that matches the visual content. This is transformative for short-form content workflows where audio post is often the most time-intensive phase.

**End-to-end control.** Sora's current iteration offers something meaningfully new: control over the entire video creation process from conceptualization through editing within a single system. That's qualitatively different from tools that only do generation.

## What Still Isn't Fixed

**Brand consistency.** If you have a brand — a specific product, a recurring talent, a recognizable visual identity — current AI video generation can't reliably maintain it across shots or sessions. Every generation is probabilistic. The product placement problem alone makes current AI video non-viable for most commercial work that involves identifiable assets.

**Director control.** The gap between "describe what you want" and "get exactly what you intended" is still enormous for complex narrative or emotional work. You can generate beautiful footage. You cannot yet reliably generate *specific* footage with the compositional control a director expects.

**Integration with existing pipelines.** The promise is AI-generated footage edited in DaVinci or Premiere alongside live action. The reality is that current AI video formats, metadata, and render specs don't play naturally with professional post-production pipelines. There's friction at every handoff.

## My Actual Use Cases in 2026

What I'm using AI video for in current production work:

**B-roll and environmental footage.** When a shoot doesn't capture an establishing shot, or when a client needs a variation that wasn't possible on location, AI-generated environmental footage closes the gap without additional production days.

**Storyboard visualization.** Generating rough visual motion for client alignment before committing to shoot days. This is where temporal consistency matters least — you're communicating intent, not delivering final footage.

**Social format variation.** Given a hero asset, AI generation can produce visual variations for different aspect ratios and motion styles. Not replacing original content creation, but multiplying the output of what gets created.

**Concept testing.** A music video director I know is using Sora to test color grade moods and lighting styles before the DP conversation. The result is faster pre-production alignment.

## The 35% Figure Is Real, And It's Not From Generation

The statistic from my Kreyol Essence work: AI-first post-production cut rough-cut turnaround by 35%. None of that came from AI video generation. It came from AI-assisted assembly — the tools that analyze footage metadata, suggest cuts based on transcript sync, and automate the mechanical work of rough cut assembly.

That's the unsexy productivity story that's actually happening in professional production right now. Not AI-generated footage replacing live-action shoots. AI-assisted editing reducing the labor cost of getting from raw media to client-ready rough cut.

The generative tools will matter more when they solve the brand consistency and pipeline integration problems. Right now, the efficiency gains are in the editing workflow, not the generation layer.

## Where I'd Bet

The inflection point I'm watching for: a major platform shipping native AI video generation *within* an existing professional NLE. When Premiere or DaVinci ship AI video generation as a timeline-native feature — not an external tool you export out of — the adoption curve changes shape.

Until then, this is a tool you add to your workflow. A powerful, increasingly useful tool. But not a workflow replacement.
